# Macro Regime Mapping Methodology

This reference expands the reusable reasoning framework behind the skill. It is designed for agents producing structured macro analysis, not for copying any individual creator’s style.

## 1. Monthly Macro Reasoning Stack

Use the following order for recurring macro reviews:

1. **Top-level changes**: identify the one to three variables that changed most since the prior review.
2. **Domestic cycle**: fiscal impulse, monetary policy, credit, inflation, property, consumption, investment, export.
3. **External cycle**: US inflation/employment/Fed, Japan fiscal-currency-bond loop, Europe energy/politics, emerging-market spillovers.
4. **Event shocks**: geopolitical conflicts, elections, tariff/legal rulings, central-bank personnel shifts, regulatory interventions.
5. **Transmission chains**: show how each variable affects prices, profits, liquidity, risk appetite, and policy.
6. **Asset map**: convert macro states into asset sensitivities.
7. **Scenario branches**: define base case, surprise case, downside case, and tail risk.
8. **Watchlist and invalidation**: specify the next evidence that would confirm or break the view.

## 2. Domestic China Framework

### Fiscal impulse

Track whether fiscal support is expanding, stable, or fading. A useful pattern is to compare:

- Central versus local government issuance
- Issuance front-loading versus later-quarter gaps
- Gross issuance versus net usable funds after debt service
- Infrastructure and fixed-asset-investment response
- Whether fiscal strength is broad or concentrated in the first quarter

If bond issuance is front-loaded, do not extrapolate first-quarter strength blindly. Ask whether later quarters will face a funding fade.

### Monetary and credit conditions

Separate liquidity abundance from real credit demand. Track:

- Rate-cut probability and policy wording
- Social financing and bank lending
- Deposit-rate adjustment
- Whether funds remain in the banking system or enter capital markets
- Whether lower rates would help households and firms borrow

A low-rate environment can coexist with weak demand. Avoid treating liquidity as a guaranteed growth recovery.

### Inflation and PPI

Decompose price changes:

- Energy shock
- Metals and upstream raw materials
- Food or seasonal factors
- Domestic demand
- Import-price pressure
- Base effects

Then ask whether upstream price increases can pass downstream. If downstream demand is weak, upstream rallies may compress margins rather than indicate broad reflation.

### Real estate

Avoid national averages only. Check:

- First-tier versus lower-tier cities
- Transaction volume versus price
- New-home supply versus second-hand market pressure
- Inventory digestion cycle
- Policy restrictions and mortgage conditions
- Whether a rebound is policy-driven, oversold repair, or organic demand recovery

Real-estate stabilization can change the entire domestic macro regime because it affects household balance sheets, collateral values, local finance, and inflation expectations.

### Three growth engines

Evaluate export, investment, and consumption separately:

- Export: trade surplus, product mix, tariff impact, non-US market access, settlement behavior
- Investment: fiscal funding, manufacturing capex, infrastructure, property investment
- Consumption: subsidies, income expectations, wealth effect, durable-goods replacement cycle

If only exports are strong while investment and consumption fade, the economy may look stable in headline GDP but remain fragile internally.

## 3. Global Framework

### United States

Track both headline data and household distribution:

- CPI/PCE and energy-adjusted inflation
- Employment headline versus revisions, sector concentration, layoffs, labor-force participation
- Household debt stress and income segmentation
- Fiscal stimulus and election incentives
- Fed reaction function and internal disagreement
- AI capex cycle, corporate credit, and private-credit risks

Do not equate strong headline employment with broad household strength. Look for K-shaped consumption and balance-sheet stress.

### Japan

Japan often operates through a fiscal-currency-bond-equity loop:

1. Fiscal expansion expectations raise bond-supply pressure.
2. Higher yields and policy expectations move the yen.
3. Yen weakness or inflation supports equity nominal growth.
4. Government and exchange reforms can lift equity valuations.
5. Currency intervention thresholds can cap extreme moves.

Watch whether equity upside is driven by real earnings, inflation pass-through, currency translation, or policy reform.

### Europe and energy-sensitive economies

For Europe, Korea, Japan, and other energy importers, energy shocks matter through:

- Import cost
- Industrial margin pressure
- Inflation expectations
- Central-bank constraints
- Political stability
- Trade competitiveness relative to energy-resilient producers

Energy shocks may be less damaging when global demand is weak or when inventories and substitution channels are available.

## 4. Geopolitical Event Analysis

Analyze geopolitical events with four layers:

1. **Physical impact**: actual supply, logistics, infrastructure, reserves, alternate routes.
2. **Financial pricing**: risk premium, leverage, positioning, volatility, safe-haven flow.
3. **Political incentives**: election deadlines, negotiation leverage, domestic approval, alliance constraints.
4. **Second-order effects**: inflation, central-bank path, trade flows, currency behavior, sector winners/losers.

Key question: is the market pricing actual economic damage or emotional uncertainty?

## 5. Asset-Class Mapping

### FX

Main drivers:

- Interest-rate differential
- Trade surplus or deficit
- Capital controls and investment channels
- Exporter settlement behavior
- Safe-haven demand
- Policy tolerance and intervention thresholds

Currency strength is often self-reinforcing until it hurts exports, exhausts settlement demand, or triggers policy resistance.

### Bonds

Main drivers:

- Inflation trend
- Growth trend
- Central-bank path
- Supply-demand balance
- Fiscal issuance
- Bank and insurance allocation demand
- Safe-haven demand

Separate short-term rate expectations from long-end supply and term premium.

### Equities

Main drivers:

- Earnings breadth
- Liquidity
- Policy support or suppression
- Leverage and retail participation
- Factor rotation
- External risk appetite
- Valuation and crowding

Rallies supported by narrow earnings or policy liquidity can reverse when the support fades.

### Commodities

Main drivers:

- Physical supply-demand
- Inventory
- Cost curve
- Financial speculation
- Geopolitical premium
- Currency and real-rate backdrop
- Downstream pass-through

Distinguish industrial commodities, precious metals, energy, and agricultural products. Precious metals can shift from macro hedge to speculative asset when leverage and retail participation dominate.

### Defensive yield assets

Main drivers:

- Dividend durability
- Bond-yield alternatives
- Regulatory stability
- Crowding
- Growth optionality
- Downside volatility

Treat dividend equities as bond-like only when cash flow and payout policy are durable.

## 6. Expectation-Gap Checklist

Look for expectation gaps with these prompts:

- What does the market currently believe?
- Which data contradicts that belief?
- What policy constraint is underappreciated?
- What second-order effect is not priced?
- Is the thesis crowded or ignored?
- What would force consensus to revise?
- What evidence would prove the contrarian view wrong?

Expectation gaps are strongest when they combine data divergence, policy incentives, and asymmetric positioning.

## 7. Scenario Table Template

| Scenario | Trigger | Macro Result | Asset Sensitivity | Invalidation |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Base case | Most likely observable path | Expected growth/inflation/liquidity outcome | FX/bond/equity/commodity sensitivity | Data or event that breaks the path |
| Upside surprise | Positive catalyst | Stronger or more benign macro outcome | Beneficiaries and laggards | Failure of catalyst |
| Downside surprise | Negative catalyst | Slower growth, tighter liquidity, or higher risk premium | Defensive and vulnerable assets | Stabilization signal |
| Tail risk | Low-probability shock | Nonlinear market or policy response | Assets with convex exposure or high loss risk | De-escalation or policy backstop |

## 8. Review and Update Loop

For recurring analysis:

1. Restate the previous thesis.
2. Mark which evidence confirmed it.
3. Mark which evidence contradicted it.
4. Update the scenario probabilities or confidence level.
5. Keep a watchlist of unresolved variables.
6. Avoid rewriting history. Clearly separate original thesis from updated thesis.

## 9. Output Quality Checklist

Before finalizing, verify:

- The analysis has a time window.
- Major claims are connected by causal chains.
- Data and narrative are separated.
- Asset implications are sensitivity-based, not direct advice.
- Scenarios include triggers and invalidation.
- High-risk instruments include explicit warnings.
- Current factual claims are cited when external sources were used.
- The wording does not imitate a specific creator or disclose source identity.

## 10. Standard Disclaimer

Macro regime mapping is an educational research framework. It does not consider the user’s personal financial situation, risk tolerance, tax position, investment restrictions, or liquidity needs. It is not financial advice.
